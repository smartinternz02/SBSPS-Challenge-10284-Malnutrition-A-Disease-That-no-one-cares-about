Underweight

What is Underweight?

Undernutrition refers to a condition where an individual's intake of essential nutrients, including calories, proteins, vitamins, and minerals, is insufficient to meet the body's energy and nutritional needs.

Underweight

Health Consequences

Undernutrition has a range of adverse health effects, particularly in children and pregnant women. It weakens the immune system, making individuals more susceptible to infections and diseases.

Underweight

Prevention and Treatment

Undernutrition is often caused by a combination of factors, including inadequate food intake, poor dietary diversity, lack of access to clean water and sanitation, limited healthcare services, and socio-economic disparities.

What is Overnutrition?

Overnutrition, also known as overeating or excessive intake of nutrients, refers to the consumption of more calories and nutrients than the body needs for its daily energy expenditure and optimal functioning.

Overnutrition

Health Consequences

Overnutrition can lead to obesity, a condition characterized by excessive body fat accumulation. Obesity is associated with a higher risk of chronic diseases, such as type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular diseases, hypertension, certain cancers, and musculoskeletal problems.

Overnutrition

Treatment and Prevention

Prevention and management of overnutrition involve promoting balanced diets that provide the right amount of calories and nutrients for an individual's age, gender, activity level, and health status.

Stunting

What is Stunting?

Stunting, also known as chronic malnutrition, is a condition characterized by impaired linear growth and short stature in children. It is typically assessed using the height-for-age Z-score (HAZ), which compares a child's height to the median height of a healthy population of the same age.

Stunting

Health Consequrences

Factors contributing to stunting include poor maternal nutrition, inadequate breastfeeding practices, lack of access to nutritious foods, poor sanitation and hygiene practices, and limited healthcare services.

Stunting

Treatment and Prevention

Prevention of stunting requires a multi-faceted approach that addresses both maternal and child nutrition. This includes promoting exclusive breastfeeding for the first six months, providing nutrient-rich complementary foods, improving maternal nutrition during pregnancy, and ensuring access to clean water, sanitation, and healthcare services.

Wasting

What is Wasting

Wasting, also known as acute malnutrition or wasting syndrome, is a condition characterized by a significant loss of muscle and fat mass, resulting in a low weight-for-height ratio.

Wasting

Health Consequences

Wasting is primarily caused by an imbalance between energy intake and energy expenditure. Inadequate food consumption, combined with factors such as illness, infections, and poor dietary diversity, can lead to rapid weight loss.

Wasting

Prevention and Treatment

Treatment for wasting involves nutritional rehabilitation and medical care. Therapeutic feeding programs, including the use of ready-to-use therapeutic foods, aim to restore a person's weight and nutritional status gradually.

Severe Wasting

What is Severe Wasting

Severe wasting is characterized by a very low weight-for-height ratio, often measured by the term "weight-for-height Z-score" (WHZ), which indicates how far a child's weight deviates from the median weight of a healthy population of the same height.

>
Severe Wasting

Health Consequences

Children with severe wasting are at a significantly increased risk of mortality. Their weakened bodies struggle to fight off common illnesses, and the combined impact of malnutrition and infection can be fatal.

Severe Wasting

Treatment and Prevention

Preventing severe wasting involves improving overall food security, access to clean water, sanitation, and healthcare services. Promoting breastfeeding, enhancing maternal nutrition, and encouraging diverse diets for infants and young children can help prevent malnutrition from developing into severe wasting.

Words From Others About Malnutrition

The United Nations Children's Fund (UNICEF) remains committed to addressing the pressing issue of malnutrition worldwide. Malnutrition poses a serious threat to children's health, growth, and development, hindering their potential for a brighter future. Our efforts are focused on delivering life-saving interventions, promoting breastfeeding, improving access to nutritious foods, and ensuring clean water and sanitation for vulnerable communities. Together with our partners, we are working tirelessly to break the cycle of malnutrition and secure a healthier world for every child.

UNICEF

The World Health Organization (WHO) recognizes that malnutrition is a complex challenge affecting populations across the globe. From undernutrition to overnutrition, this issue poses significant health risks and socioeconomic burdens. Our approach involves advocating for evidence-based policies, supporting health systems to provide adequate nutrition services, and fostering international collaboration. By addressing malnutrition comprehensively, we strive to reduce the global burden of disease, enhance resilience, and promote well-being for all.

WHO

UNESCO recognizes that malnutrition not only impacts health but also hinders human development, education, and cultural preservation. Malnutrition can impede cognitive development, hinder learning outcomes, and perpetuate cycles of poverty. As we strive for inclusive and equitable education, we emphasize the importance of addressing malnutrition to ensure that every child has the opportunity to thrive. Additionally, safeguarding traditional knowledge, cultural practices, and indigenous food systems contributes to addressing malnutrition sustainably. Through collaboration with partners, UNESCO remains committed to advancing holistic solutions that promote well-being, learning, and cultural heritage preservation.
